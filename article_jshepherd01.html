Empathy: the Final Frontier

As a computer science student, writing articles is not even slightly my thing. If I wanted to write words for humans all the time, I would be doing an English degree. One thing I can do though, is make computers do things for me.

One such computer is the server at inferkit.com, which contains an artificial intelligence designed to spout something that isn't quite gibberish in response to a prompt. I gave it this article's title, and got it to write one for me.

So here, in the words of app.inferkit.com/demo, is Empathy: the Final Frontier:

Many science fiction books make time travel possible, but what if, say, the event that caused you to travel back to the past was not due to time travel, but rather, a little empathy?

We all have friends who have made decisions that might have caused other people to lose their jobs. No matter how small these situations may seem, they may have a huge impact on the people involved. The knowledge of what's going on, without being able to do anything, is much more than a simple headache.

Researchers are challenging our accepted notions of how the human brain actually works. ‘We’re now turning to modelling as a computational tool,’ says Tony Kraljevic from Northwestern University, explaining that existing experimental neuroscience methods ‘turn out to be very limited’.

All this means scientists now need to come up with new experimental design to find the neural circuits responsible for all the cognitive and emotional behaviours.

Empathy: the Final Frontier by Charlotte Gearing sounds like it might be a really great concept that would explain things very well.

Arkanoid 2 by Atari: what more can I say except that Atari is giving the sequel of a 1986 classic and that it's on my list?

Various Arcade Hits Pack 3 by Devolver Digital: this is a great collection of great arcade games, which means I'm still playing it after nearly two years. Maybe Devolver can come up with another monthly pack like this in the future?

Well, that was interesting. As far as I can tell, Tony Kraljevic does not exist. Apart from that, this was surprisingly coherent. Huh.
